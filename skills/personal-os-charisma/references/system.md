### Mastery — The Craft Development Path

Greene's 3-phase mastery model applied to product entrepreneurship:

1. **Apprenticeship** (0-2 years): Learn the industry deeply. Work with/for others in the category. Build skill, not brand.
2. **Creative-Active** (2-5 years): Develop your unique angle. Combine disciplines others keep separate. Create distinctive product.
3. **Mastery** (5+ years): Intuitive understanding of market. Pattern recognition across situations. Teaching others amplifies authority.

## Negotiation Deep Dive

### Voss's Tactical Empathy — Step-by-Step

**Before the negotiation:**
1. Research their pressures, constraints, and timeline
2. Prepare an Accusation Audit (list every negative thing they might think about you)
3. Define your target, your ask, and your walk-away
4. Prepare 3 calibrated questions that start with "How" or "What"

**During the negotiation:**
1. Open with the Accusation Audit to defuse tension
2. Use Mirroring (repeat last 1-3 words) to encourage elaboration
3. Use Labeling ("It seems like...", "It sounds like...") to validate emotions
4. Ask calibrated questions to give them illusion of control
5. Push for "That's right" — not "You're right"
6. Use strategic silence (pause 3-5 seconds after they speak)

**Closing:**
1. Summarize their position until they say "That's right"
2. Present your proposal as solving THEIR stated problem
3. Use the Ackerman model for price negotiation: 65% → 85% → 95% → 100% of target

### Fisher/Ury BATNA Analysis Template

Before any negotiation, complete this:

| Element | Your Side | Their Side |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Best Alternative to Agreement | What's your next best option? | What's their next best option? |
| Reservation Price | Absolute minimum you'll accept | Maximum they'll pay (estimate) |
| Zone of Possible Agreement | Where your ranges overlap | |
| Interests (not positions) | What do you REALLY need? | What do THEY really need? |
| Objective criteria | Industry standards, market rates | |

## Mental Model Catalog (Extended)

### Decision Quality Models

| Model | Definition | Product Seller Example |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Inversion** | Think backwards from failure | "What would make customers never buy again?" → avoid those things |
| **First Principles** | Strip to fundamentals | "What does my customer ACTUALLY need?" (not "what does the industry sell") |
| **Second-Order Effects** | What happens AFTER the first effect? | "Cutting price → more sales → lower brand perception → harder to raise prices" |
| **Reversibility** | Is this decision reversible? | Reversible → decide fast. Irreversible → decide carefully. |
| **Regret Minimization** | Which choice will you regret less at 80? | Bezos framework for big career decisions |

### Systems Thinking Models

| Model | Definition | Application |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **Feedback Loops** | Reinforcing (growth) or balancing (limiting) | Good reviews → more sales → more reviews (reinforcing). High growth → quality drops → bad reviews (balancing) |
| **Bottleneck** | System is limited by its weakest link | Find THE constraint: is it traffic? Conversion? Retention? Fix that ONE thing |
| **Leverage Points** | Small changes with big effects | Email list is leverage: 1 email → thousands of impressions at near-zero cost |
| **Emergence** | System properties that parts don't have | Brand = product + story + community + distribution — none alone is the brand |

### Competitive Strategy Models

| Model | Definition | Application |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **Blue Ocean** | Create uncontested market space | Don't compete on "better skincare" — create "skincare for [specific situation]" |
| **Moats** | Sustainable competitive advantages | Brand loyalty, proprietary process, distribution lock-in, network effects |
| **Disruption** | Start at bottom of market, move up | Offer "good enough" product at dramatically lower price to ignored segment |
| **Barbell Strategy** | Extreme safety + extreme risk | 80% proven products + 20% experimental products |

## Network Architecture

### Burt's Structural Holes

Ronald Burt's research: The most powerful position in a network is **bridging groups that don't communicate with each other**. These "structural holes" create information advantages.

**For product sellers:**
- Bridge manufacturer networks and consumer communities
- Bridge different industry verticals (e.g., food + beauty if your product spans both)
- Bridge domestic and international markets
- Bridge online and offline retail communities

### Building Bridging Capital

| Action | Bridges | Frequency |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Attend cross-industry events | Different verticals | Monthly |
| Join international trade groups | Domestic ↔ International | Quarterly |
| Contribute to both creator and business communities | Creator ↔ Commercial | Weekly |
| Connect suppliers with retailers | Upstream ↔ Downstream | As opportunities arise |

### Adam Grant's Give and Take

Long-term givers outperform takers and matchers. But only **strategic givers** — those who give with boundaries.

**Giver Protocol:**
1. Give value without expecting immediate return
2. Set boundaries: say no to requests that drain without building
3. Give to other givers and matchers; limit giving to takers
4. Make giving visible (not secretly) — it builds reputation
5. Five-minute favors: if it takes < 5 minutes and helps someone, always do it
