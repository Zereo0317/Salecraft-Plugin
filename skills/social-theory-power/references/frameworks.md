# Social Theory & Power -- Complete Framework Reference

This reference contains the full theoretical foundation for the `social-theory-power` skill. Load this when performing deep structural analysis of a market or competitive landscape.

---

## Framework 1: Classical Trio -- Marx, Weber, Durkheim

### 1A. Marx -- Ideology & Commodity Fetishism

**Core concept**: The ruling class shapes what people consider "normal" and "good." In markets, dominant brands define the category standards, aesthetics, and value metrics -- everyone else plays by their rules.

**Commodity Fetishism**: Consumers see the finished product but not the labor, materials, or exploitation behind it. A $200 face cream appears as pure luxury; the $3 raw material cost and factory conditions are invisible.

**Brand application**:
- **Expose what incumbents hide**: If big brands use synthetic ingredients but market as "natural," a small brand can weaponize transparency. Show the factory. Name the farmers. Reveal the supply chain.
- **De-fetishize the category**: "You're not paying for the cream -- you're paying for the TV ad." This is the core playbook of DTC brands like Everlane ("Radical Transparency") and Warby Parker.
- **Re-fetishize with your own narrative**: After exposing the incumbent's fetish, create a new one. Instead of "luxury = expensive packaging," redefine luxury as "luxury = you know every ingredient by name."

**Diagnostic questions**:
- What does the industry narrative hide? (ingredient sourcing? labor conditions? markup structure?)
- What would happen if consumers saw the full production chain?
- Can the user's transparency become a competitive weapon?

**Practical example -- skincare industry**:
A small organic skincare brand competes against L'Oreal. Marx's framework reveals: L'Oreal's power comes from defining "effective skincare" as "scientifically advanced" (their narrative). The small brand's counter: redefine "effective" as "ingredient transparency + traceable sourcing." The commodity fetishism break: "Their 'patented complex' is 0.1% of the formula. Here's our full ingredient list with sourcing."

### 1B. Weber -- Three Authority Types

**Core concept**: People obey authority for three distinct reasons. Each maps to a brand trust-building strategy.

| Authority Type | Source | Brand Strategy | Example |
|----------------|--------|----------------|--------|
| **Traditional** | "This is how it's always been done" | Heritage, origin story, generational craft | "Third-generation tea master since 1952" |
| **Charismatic** | Personal magnetism of a leader | Founder-led brand, KOL partnership, personality-driven | Elon Musk for Tesla; founder-face brands |
| **Legal-Rational** | Rules, certifications, data | SGS certified, FDA approved, clinical trials, patent | "Clinically proven 47% improvement in 28 days" |

**Brand application**:
- **New brands with no history**: Cannot use traditional authority. Must choose charismatic (founder story) or legal-rational (certifications/data).
- **Brands with heritage**: Double down on traditional authority. "100-year recipe" is powerful in food, tea, spirits, textiles.
- **Brands in skeptical categories** (health supplements, anti-aging): Legal-rational is mandatory. Without third-party validation, charisma alone triggers skepticism.
- **Combination strategy**: The strongest brands stack 2-3 types. Apple: charismatic (Jobs legacy) + legal-rational (engineering excellence) + quasi-traditional (design lineage).

**Diagnostic questions**:
- Which authority type does the user currently have? (Even partially?)
- Which authority type do competitors rely on?
- What is the fastest path to credible authority? (Certification takes 3 months; founder story takes 1 week; heritage cannot be manufactured)

### 1C. Durkheim -- Anomie & Collective Rituals

**Core concept**: When old social norms break down (anomie), people seek new rituals and communities to belong to. Brands that provide rituals win loyalty beyond product quality.

**Brand application**:
- **Create purchase rituals**: Nespresso's capsule selection ritual. Apple's unboxing experience. Lush's in-store sensory ritual.
- **Build community as antidote to anomie**: Modern consumers are atomized (Putnam's "Bowling Alone"). Brands that create belonging outperform those that just sell products.
- **Seasonal/cyclical rituals**: Limited editions, annual releases, membership renewal moments.
- **Physical product rituals**: Tea ceremony around your tea brand. Skincare "evening ritual" routine. Cooking "weekend recipe challenge" with your ingredients.

**Diagnostic questions**:
- Does the user's product have a natural ritual moment? (morning coffee, evening skincare, weekend cooking)
- Can the user create a community ritual? (monthly tasting, seasonal release, user meetup)
- What social norm is breaking down in the user's category? (e.g., fast fashion fatigue = anomie = opportunity for slow fashion ritual)

## Framework 2: Foucault -- Discipline, Biopolitics, Governmentality

**Core concepts**:
- **Panopticon**: Power works not through punishment but through the feeling of being watched. People self-regulate.
- **Biopolitics**: Power manages populations through health, hygiene, wellness norms.
- **Governmentality**: Power shapes what people want, not just what they do. "Power not by violence but by knowledge/norms/self-management."

**Brand application**:

### Platform as Panopticon
Every platform (Amazon, Shopee, Instagram, TikTok) is a panopticon. Sellers self-regulate to please the algorithm: posting at "optimal times," using "trending sounds," optimizing for "engagement rate." This is disciplinary power -- the platform never explicitly forces compliance, but sellers internalize the rules.

**Strategic response**:
- **Recognize the panopticon**: Name it. "We're all dancing for the algorithm." This awareness prevents wasted effort on metric-chasing.
- **Multi-platform insurance**: Never depend on a single panopticon. Own channels (website, LINE, email list) are panopticon-free zones.
- **Exploit the watchers**: The algorithm watches patterns. Become the pattern it rewards: consistency > virality.

### Biopolitics in Product Marketing
The wellness industry is pure biopolitics: "You should eat organic," "You need SPF 50," "Your gut microbiome needs probiotics." These aren't neutral facts -- they're power structures that create markets.

**Strategic response**:
- **Ride the biopolicy wave**: If the prevailing health narrative supports the product (e.g., "clean beauty"), align with it. Don't fight the norm; surf it.
- **Create micro-norms**: "The 7-step skincare routine" is a biopolitical invention by the Korean beauty industry. It created demand for 7 products instead of 2. The user can create their own micro-norm.
- **Counter-biopolitics**: If the norm hurts the product (e.g., "carbs are bad" for a bakery), challenge the norm with science and story.

### Governmentality -- Shaping Desire
The most powerful brands don't convince you to buy; they shape what you want. Apple didn't sell phones; it redefined what a phone should be. A small brand can do the same at category level.

**Strategic application**: Instead of "buy our organic soap," reframe: "You deserve to know what touches your skin every day." The user didn't ask for organic soap -- the brand reshaped what the user wants (knowledge about ingredients).

## Framework 3: Bourdieu -- Cultural Capital, Habitus, Field, Symbolic Violence

### Cultural Capital
Three forms:
1. **Embodied**: Knowledge, taste, refinement you carry in your body. The wine connoisseur's palate. The designer's eye.
2. **Objectified**: Cultural goods that signal status. Books, art, specific brands.
3. **Institutionalized**: Degrees, certifications, titles.

**Brand application**:
- **Map the taste hierarchy**: Every product category has "high culture" and "low culture." In coffee: third-wave single-origin (high) vs instant coffee (low). In skincare: dermatologist-recommended (high) vs drugstore (low).
- **Position within or against the hierarchy**: Either climb the existing hierarchy (get the right certifications, aesthetics, price point) or create a new one (craft beer didn't join the wine hierarchy; it created its own).
- **Transfer cultural capital**: Partner with entities that have the cultural capital the user lacks. A new skincare brand partners with a dermatologist (institutionalized capital) or a fashion magazine (objectified capital).

### Habitus -- Class Embodied
Habitus is the set of dispositions, tastes, and behaviors people acquire through their social class. It's why certain customers "just feel" that a brand is "for them" or "not for them."

**Brand application**:
- **Match the habitus**: Design packaging, language, imagery, and price point to match target customer's habitus. A premium tea brand targeting educated professionals uses minimalist design, literary references, and serif fonts -- because that's what this habitus "feels right" about.
- **Challenge the habitus**: Streetwear brands deliberately violate luxury habitus (oversized, graphic, loud) to create new cultural capital for a different class.

### Symbolic Violence
The dominant class imposes its taste as "universal truth." Organic food is "better" -- but that's a class-coded value judgment that excludes people who can't afford it. Luxury brands commit symbolic violence by defining "good taste" as their taste.

**Brand application**:
- **Expose symbolic violence**: "Who decided that good skincare has to cost $200?" This is a positioning weapon for affordable quality brands.
- **Commit strategic symbolic violence**: Premium brands deliberately exclude. "Not for everyone" is symbolic violence that creates desirability. Use with caution and only for genuine premium products.

## Framework 4: Media Theory -- McLuhan, Postman, Habermas

### McLuhan -- "The Medium Is the Message"
The platform shapes the content more than the content itself. A product review on TikTok (60s video, music, cuts) communicates fundamentally differently from the same review on a blog (1500 words, images, links).

**Brand application**:
- **Choose the medium that amplifies the product's strength**: Visually stunning product? Instagram/Pinterest. Complex product needing explanation? YouTube/blog. Impulse-buy product? TikTok/Reels. Relationship-heavy product? LINE/WhatsApp.
- **Don't force content into wrong medium**: A 2000-word ingredient analysis doesn't belong on TikTok. A 15-second dance doesn't sell premium consulting.

### Postman -- Amusing Ourselves to Death
All public discourse trends toward entertainment. Serious product information gets drowned by entertaining content. The attention economy rewards spectacle over substance.

**Brand application**:
- **Edutainment**: Combine education with entertainment. Don't choose between "fun" and "informative" -- do both. The 1.5-second hook requirement is real; but the substance must follow.
- **Long-form as differentiation**: In a sea of 15-second content, a 10-minute deep-dive video builds authority precisely because it's rare. The medium signals seriousness.

### Habermas -- Public Sphere
An ideal space for rational discourse where the best argument wins, regardless of status. The internet was supposed to be this. Instead, we got echo chambers and outrage algorithms.

**Brand application**:
- **Create a micro-public-sphere**: The user's community (LINE group, Facebook group, newsletter) can be a rational discourse space about the product category. This builds trust in a way advertising never can.
- **Facilitate dialogue, not broadcast**: Brands that listen and respond (real customer Q&A, transparent complaint handling) create public sphere moments.

## Framework 5: Network Society -- Castells, Zuboff, Tufekci

### Castells -- Network Power
In network society, **switchers** (those who connect different networks) have the most power. A KOL who connects the beauty community with the sustainability community is a switcher.

**Brand application**:
- **Become a switcher**: Connect two communities that don't normally talk. A food brand that connects health-conscious consumers with traditional farmers is a switcher.
- **Identify and recruit switchers**: Find KOLs, communities, or platforms that bridge the user's target audience with adjacent audiences.
- **Network position > network size**: 1,000 followers in a bridge position > 100,000 followers in a saturated cluster.

### Zuboff -- Surveillance Capitalism
Platforms extract behavioral data as raw material for prediction products. Sellers on Amazon/Shopee/Instagram are simultaneously users and products -- their sales data trains the platform's recommendation engine and competitor intelligence.

**Brand application**:
- **Data awareness**: Know what data the platform extracts and how it's used. Amazon uses seller data to launch Amazon Basics competitors.
- **Privacy as differentiator**: "We don't track you. We don't sell your data. We don't retarget you." In an era of surveillance fatigue, this is a positioning weapon.
- **Own the data**: Build direct customer relationships (email, LINE, membership) to reduce platform data dependency.

### Tufekci -- Tactical Freedom / Strategic Weakness Paradox
Social media gives tactical power (anyone can go viral) but strategic weakness (no institutional infrastructure for sustained action). A small brand can get a viral TikTok but can't sustain the attention without systems.

**Brand application**:
- **Viral is tactical, not strategic**: Plan for what happens after virality. Capture emails. Build community. Create repeat purchase mechanisms.
- **Invest in strategic infrastructure**: CRM, email sequences, membership programs, referral systems -- the boring stuff that converts tactical wins into strategic position.

## Framework 6: AGI Era -- Acemoglu, Varoufakis

### Acemoglu -- Technology Doesn't Auto-Benefit the Masses
Technological progress concentrates wealth unless institutions redirect gains. AI-powered marketing tools benefit those who can afford and understand them -- widening the gap between sophisticated and unsophisticated sellers.

**Brand application**:
- **AI as equalizer (if used right)**: Tools like SaleCraft exist to give small sellers access to marketing intelligence that was previously agency-only. But the user must actively use them, not just have access.
- **Institutional positioning**: The user's "institution" is their brand system (SOPs, playbooks, customer data). Build it deliberately.

### Varoufakis -- Technofeudalism
Platforms are the new feudal lords. Sellers are digital serfs paying rent (commissions, ad spend) to access customers that the platform "owns." The platform extracts rent without producing value.

**Brand application**:
- **Reduce feudal dependency**: Every dollar spent building owned channels (website, email list, LINE) reduces rent paid to platform lords.
- **Accept strategic feudalism**: Sometimes paying platform rent is worth it for customer acquisition. But always have an exit strategy.
- **Coalition building**: Seller communities, industry associations, and cross-brand collaborations reduce individual vulnerability to platform power.
